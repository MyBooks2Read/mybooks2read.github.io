300 CHAPTER 9 The geometry of vector algebra 9.4.3 Practical example: Distance to a plane Let’s say we have a 3D plane set by a triplet of points. We assume that the points don’t belong to the same line, so one and only one plane hosts them all. Now let’s say we have the fourth point, and we want to know the distance from this point to the afore- mentioned plane. Then we could use this distance in some practical application. In chapter 10, for example, we’ll use it to determine the distance field to the whole trian- gle mesh. p 1 = (x 1 , y 1 , z 1 ) p 2 = (x 2 , y 2 , z 2 ) p 2 = (x 3 , y 3 , z 3 ) p = (x, y, z) The most straightforward solution would be to Google “three-point plane equation,” get the normal vector from the plane, project the fourth point on the normal vector sticking out of the plane’s point of origin, and then get the distance from this projec- tion to the plane’s origin. A more elegant solution would be to form three vectors: two that make a parallelo- gram on a triplet’s plane and a third one that adds to them to form a parallelepiped in 3D (figure 9.20). The signed volume of the parallelepiped is the triple product (p 2 – p 1 ) × (p 3 – p 1 ) · (p – p 1 ). The area of the parallelogram is the length of the cross product (p 2 – p 1 ) × (p 3 – p 1 ). The height of the parallelepiped, which is its volume divided by the area of the base, is the distance we’re looking for.
