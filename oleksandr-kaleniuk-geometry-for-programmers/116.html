92 CHAPTER 4 Projective geometric transformations As a continuous odd function, the sine goes through the zero point. It has to. If x = 0 is anything else but 0—let’s say c —the function would have to jump immediately to –c on the other side, which would break its continuity (figure 4.7). The only number that survives negation without change is zero: 0 = –0. Here’s a little bonus for performance enthusiasts. Not only sine of zero is zero, but also, for small values of x, the sine of x is approximately x itself: sin(x) ≈ x. This means that for the small xs, the sin(x) function can be replaced by the much cheaper f (x) = x. For x = 0.244, sin(x) ≈ 0.242, so the relative difference is about 1%, which also means that for x < 0.244, the relative difference is even less than that. Figure 4.8 shows the cosine.
