Now we can take our two-trains problem from before and draw it (fig- ure 3.1): V p – 2V a = 0 V a + V p – 450 = 0 The solution is immediately apparent: It’s where the lines intersect. In figure 3.1, the solution is the point (300, 150). We can do the same thing in 3D. A linear equation like this ax + by + cz = d turns into a plane equation: ax + by + cz – d = 0 A system of three variables, therefore, is a system of three 3D planes. The point of the solution is where all the planes intersect. We don’t want to stop at equations with only two or three variables, of course. But to keep the geometrical sense as we add more variables, we need a new term, a new geometrical concept that generalizes lines and planes, and that corresponds to a lin- ear equation of N variables: a 1 x 1 + a 2 x 2 + … + a N x N = b Figure 3.1 The two-trains problem as an intersection of lines
