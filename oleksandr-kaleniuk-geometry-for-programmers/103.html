Because we’re hunting for the ray-triangle intersection, we want to filter out the points that don’t belong to the triangle. Luckily, in a triangle’s own basis, this task is easy (figure 3.17). In figure 3.17, the triangle is parametrized in u and v coordinates, where u goes along the AB vector and v goes along the AC vector. Then the equation and constraints that specify all the points on a triangle are S = A + ABu + ACv, u ≥ 0, v ≥ 0, u + v ≤ 1 The constraints on u and v guarantee that the point lies on the triangle. The equation for the right border is u = 0, and v = 0 constitutes the bottom border; u + v = 1 is the same as v = 1 – u; and this is the equation for the line that goes diagonally from (0, 1) to (1, 0). Now let’s form the system for a ray-triangle intersection. We use R to denote a point on a ray and S to denote a point on a triangle, so a ray hits a triangle if and only if R = S, respecting all the parametric constraints.
